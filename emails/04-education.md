Investors ask me all the time: "How do you know if a deal is actually good?"

Here are the 5 metrics I evaluate before I'd recommend any commercial property to a client — in order of importance.

**1. Cap Rate (but in context)**
A 6% cap on a Dollar General in a growing corridor is different from a 6% cap on a local tenant in a declining market. I always look at cap rate relative to the tenant credit, lease term, and location growth trajectory. Current Orlando ranges: NNN 4.5–6.5%, Retail 6.0–8.5%, Industrial 5.25–6.50%, Multifamily 5.0–6.5%.

**2. DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio)**
This tells you whether the property's income comfortably covers the mortgage. I want to see 1.25x minimum — meaning the property generates 25% more income than the debt payment requires. Below 1.20x and you're one vacancy away from feeding the property out of pocket.

**3. Tenant Credit Quality**
Who's paying the rent matters as much as how much. Investment-grade tenants (rated BBB- or better) give you predictable income. A 7% cap with a local tenant carries more risk than a 5.5% cap with Walgreens — but the right local tenant in the right location can be the better return.

**4. Lease Term Remaining**
A property with 12 years remaining on a NNN lease is a very different asset than one with 2 years left. Short remaining term means re-tenanting risk and potential capital expenditure. I generally want 7+ years for passive NNN, and I factor in renewal option likelihood.

**5. Cash-on-Cash Return**
This is your actual return on the cash you invested — after debt service. It accounts for leverage, which cap rate alone doesn't. I target 7–10%+ for stabilized deals. Below 7%, the deal needs to have clear value-add upside or appreciation potential to justify the capital.

**The metric I don't rely on: Price alone.**
A $900K property isn't "cheaper" than a $2.5M property if the $900K deal has a 1.05x DSCR and a local tenant with 2 years left on the lease. Always evaluate the income, not the sticker price.
